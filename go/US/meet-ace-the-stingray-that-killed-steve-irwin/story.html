The Stingray Named Ace: The Creature that Killed Steve Irwin

A moment that changed the world of biology forever.

ByJanice McDonald and Ivan Pereira
August 23, 2018, 4:33 PM

In the world of wildlife conservation and television, few figures have had as profound an impact as Steve Irwin. Known as "The Crocodile Hunter," Irwin captivated millions with his fearless approach to animal encounters, his deep love for wildlife, and his commitment to conservation. Tragically, on September 4, 2006, Irwin's life was cut short after a fatal encounter with a stingray while filming a documentary on the Great Barrier Reef.

While the name of the stingray that caused Irwin's death is often remembered, there is an air of mystery and intrigue surrounding the creature, particularly given the heightened attention it received after the incident. In the years following, it was revealed that the stingray responsible for Steve Irwins death was a large marine stingray (also known as a "bull ray")a species whose behavior and temperament are typically non-aggressive. The stingray in question was affectionately named "Ace" by those close to the situation.

On the day of the incident, Steve Irwin was filming a documentary called Ocean's Deadliest at the Great Barrier Reef. He and his crew were swimming in shallow waters near Low Isles when they encountered the stingray. Irwin, as always, was keen to educate the public about marine life, displaying a sense of excitement as he interacted with the stingray.

However, in a tragic twist, as Irwin was filming with the stingray in relatively shallow water, the animal became startled. In a defensive response, the stingray turned and jabbed its barbed tail into Irwin's chest. The stingray's tail barb pierced Irwin's heart, and despite efforts to resuscitate him, he was pronounced dead shortly thereafter.

The incident was both shocking and heartbreaking, given Irwin's extensive experience with dangerous animals and his reputation for handling them with caution and respect. It was a reminder of the inherent unpredictability of wild animals and the risks that come with such close interactions.

In the wake of Steve Irwins tragic death, the stingray that caused the fatal injury was identified as an Indo-Pacific bull ray (Himantura uarnak). It was not a particularly rare or aggressive species, but it had a defense mechanism that could be deadly under certain circumstances. This specific stingray was affectionately named "Ace" by the team involved in the filming.

Despite the emotional connection many fans felt to Irwin, there was no desire to vilify the stingray. Experts and those who knew the situation understood that the stingray had acted out of instinct, not malice. Stingrays, like many wild creatures, are generally peaceful and would much rather avoid confrontation. The unfortunate sequence of events was simply an accident, as Ace had felt threatened by the presence of Irwin and his crew.

Stingrays, especially those found in the waters of Australia, are known for their flat bodies and long, whip-like tails, which can carry venomous barbs. The barbs, which are used primarily for defense against predators, are capable of inflicting serious injury if the stingray feels threatened. When a stingray is startled or cornered, it may lash out defensively, leading to the type of incident that led to Irwins tragic death.

The stingrays venom is not necessarily fatal, but the barbs can cause severe injury, and when a sting strikes vital organs like the heart, the result can be catastrophic. In Steve Irwins case, the barb punctured his chest near his heart, and despite efforts by his crew to save him, the damage was too severe.

In the years since, Steve Irwin's legacy has lived on through the Australia Zoo and the conservation efforts of his family. The Stingray that killed Steve Irwin, "Ace," was ultimately not vilified but understood within the broader context of wildlife behavior and the respect that Irwin always promoted. Ace is now seen by many as an accidental part of a much larger story a story of love for animals, commitment to their conservation, and the importance of human responsibility in a wild world.
